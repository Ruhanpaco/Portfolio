# Volunteering at DevFest Kosova 2025: Chaos, Community, and Code

The doors weren't even fully open when the first wave hit. Over 500 developers, students, and tech enthusiasts had been waiting outside the University of Prishtina, and the moment we unlocked the entrance, it became clear that our four-person check-in team wasn't going to cut it. Within minutes, we had to call in five more volunteers just to handle the overflow.

I was stationed at the registration desk, scanning QR codes as fast as I could while trying to answer questions in three different languages. The line stretched back through the lobby and out into the courtyard. People were excited, impatient, and ready to get inside. This was **DevFest Kosova 2025**, hosted by **Google Developer Group Prishtina**, and it was already living up to its reputation as the largest tech event in the Balkans.

## Getting Here

Months earlier, I'd applied to volunteer without really knowing what I was getting into. The application process turned out to be surprisingly competitive—over 50 people applied for a handful of spots. I submitted my application a few days after the application opend had already passed, and when Gjon Co-founder of gdg prishtina, told me "the chances are slim," I figured that was that.

But I'd spent the past year building **EliteDev Agency** and working with local tech communities especially with gdg prishtina, so I sent in my application anyway, highlighting that experience. When the acceptance email came through, the adrenaline was immediate. I had no idea I'd be thrown into the deep end quite so literally.

## The Check-In Chaos

The first real test came at 8 AM when we opened the doors. We'd prepared for a steady flow of arrivals throughout the morning. What we got instead was everyone showing up at once.

The check-in system worked like this: attendees had QR codes on their phones, we scanned them, verified their registration, and told them were to go. Simple enough in theory. In practice, with hundreds of people pushing forward, phones dying, QR codes not loading, and last-minute registrations coming through, it became a full-contact sport.

I remember one moment clearly: I was processing someone's check-in when three more people leaned over the desk simultaneously, all asking different questions. Behind them, the line had split into two unofficial queues, and volunteers were shouting to each other across the lobby trying to coordinate who was handling what. Someone's phone fell, spilling stickers everywhere.

We adapted fast. Two volunteers became dedicated "line managers," keeping people organized and answering questions before they reached the desk. Another handled the edge cases—people who'd registered under different names, groups trying to check in together, sponsors who needed special badges. I stayed on QR code duty, scanning as fast as the system would let me.

By 9:30 AM, we'd processed most of the crowd. My hands were cramping from holding my iphone and also typing so fest on my laptop to people who didnt register, and I'd answered "yes, there's coffee inside" at least forty times. But we'd done it. The main hall was packed, the opening keynote was about to start, and somehow, everyone didnt't have the badges becuase one of the partner that was given to print the badges failed to make them in time, so we were in panic until they came.

## Inside the Event

Once check-in settled down, I got to see what we'd been working so hard to make happen. DevFest Kosova 2025 ran across two days—Day 1 at FIEK (University of Prishtina) and Day 2 at RIT Kosovo—with an incredible lineup that brought together engineers from Google, Microsoft, Cloudflare, Intel, and more.

### Day 1: The Keynotes

Day 1 kicked off at 3 PM with **Kyle Bringmans** (Software Engineer III at Google) talking about "Life at Google and What It Takes to Join." This was one of my favorite sessions—Kyle broke down the actual interview process, the skills Google looks for, and what it's really like working there. The room was packed, and I found myself stopping my volunteer duties just to listen. Everyone wanted to hear what it actually takes to work at one of the big tech companies.

**Yoyu Li** (Creative Technology Director at Infinite Whys Ltd) ran a workshop on "Building an AI Game with Agent Development Kit" that had people completely absorbed. Watching developers build an actual AI game in real-time was incredible.

But the session that really stuck with me was **Dajana Stojchevska's** (Senior Software Engineer at Google) talk on "Beyond Coding – 10 Lessons You Won't Learn in School." She talked about communication, dealing with ambiguity, understanding business context—the real skills you need in tech beyond just writing code. You could see people frantically taking notes. This was exactly the kind of practical advice that makes conferences valuable.

The day wrapped up with a panel discussion on "From Hype to Production: Real-World AI Challenges" featuring **Adrian Romanski**, **Gentrit Fazlija**, and **Celik Nimani**. The questions from the audience kept coming—people wanted to know how to actually implement AI in production, not just the theory.

### Day 2: Deep Dives and Workshops

Day 2 at RIT Kosovo was even more intense. **Igor Minar** (Senior Director of Engineering at Cloudflare) and **Natalia Venditto** (Principal Product Manager at Microsoft) opened with a keynote on "Web Fragments: Micro-Frontends Done Right?" The technical depth was impressive.

Throughout the day, I was running between helping coordinate logistics and trying to catch sessions. **Federico Iezzi** from Google talked about "Scaling Inference to Billions of Users and Agents," and **Seifeddin Mansri** (Cloud CTO at SFEIR) gave a talk on FinOps that had everyone rethinking their cloud costs. **Miraç Vuslat Başaran** (Senior Software Engineer at Google) delivered an important session on "Privacy: What Every Developer Needs to Know," while **Enes Turan** from IBM covered "Building Intelligent Agents: AI Architectures and Vertex AI Landscape."

The workshops were incredible. One of my absolute favorites was **Vjollca Kastrati's** (Quality Assurance Manager at Wasabi Technologies) workshop on "Root Cause Thinking – Beyond the Bug." She showed how to think systematically about problems instead of just fixing symptoms. It completely changed how I think about debugging.

**Bilal Çınarlı** (Principal Software Engineer at Atlassian) ran a packed session on "Frontend Efficiency & Scaling Teams," while **Gentrit Fazlija** (Co-Founder and CTO at AI Strategy Institute, also an NVIDIA AI Advisor) led "Building Agents in the Real World: From Idea to Workflow." **Hasan Cana** from Wasabi taught "DAL: The Contract Between Your Code and Your Data," and **Matthias Geisler** from Airalo covered "Test-Driven Development for Beginners with Kotlin." Watching developers actually build things in real-time, hitting bugs, and solving them together—that's what made it special.

The sponsor booths from **Wasabi Technologies** and **Kode Labs** were constantly busy. I'd run past and see **Hasan Cana**, **Faton Mustafa**, and **Vjollca Kastrati** from Wasabi deep in technical discussions with attendees. **Gentrit Gojani** from Kode Labs was always surrounded by people asking about their platform.

Between sessions, the hallways were packed. During the lunch breaks, instead of the usual conference small talk, people were sketching out architecture diagrams on napkins and pulling up code on their laptops to show each other. The energy never really dipped.

## What Stuck

By the time we were breaking down the venue on Day 2, I was completely exhausted. My feet hurt from running between venues, my voice was hoarse from answering questions and coordinating with other volunteers, and I'd consumed more coffee than was probably advisable.

But I'd also just been part of something incredible. Working alongside **Kujtesa Pacarizi** (Founder & Lead Organizer), **Gjon Hajdari** (Organizer & Creative Director), **Getoar Krasniqi**, and **Alba Hajdini**—the core team behind GDG Prishtina—I got to see firsthand what it takes to pull off the largest tech event in the Balkans. These weren't just organizers; they were my friends, and watching them coordinate everything from speaker logistics to last-minute venue changes was inspiring.

The chaos at check-in, the packed sessions with engineers from Google and Microsoft, the workshops where people actually built things, the conversations in the hallways about real technical challenges—it all added up to exactly what a tech conference should be: messy, energetic, and full of people who genuinely cared about what they were building.

The fact that over 500 people showed up, that we had speakers flying in from around the world, that **Wasabi Technologies** and **Kode Labs** believed in the event enough to sponsor it—all of that happened because of the GDG Prishtina team's vision and execution.

Would I do it again? Absolutely. Though next time, I'm requesting at least seven people on the check-in team from the start, and I'm making sure the badge printing partner confirms their delivery time twice.
